DDoS Attacks

A distributed denial-of-service (DDoS) attack is a malicious attempt to disrupt the normal traffic of a targeted server, service or network by overwhelming the target or its surrounding infrastructure with a flood of internet traffic. DDoS attacks achieve effectiveness by exploiting multiple machines and computer systems as sources of attack traffic.

DDoS attacks are carried out with networks of Internet-connected machines that have been infected with malware, allowing them to be controlled remotely by the attacker. These individual devices are known as bots, and a group of bots is called a botnet. Once a botnet has been made, the attacker is able to direct an attack by sending remote instructions to each bot.

Why Are DDoS Attacks Performed?

  • Financial Gain: Ransom demands in return for stopping the attack.
  • Revenge or Activism: Disruption of services for ideological or political reasons.
  • Competition: Sabotaging rival businesses by making their services unavailable.
  • Testing Security: Malicious actors testing their capabilities or tools.

Identifying a DDoS Attack

The most obvious symptom of a DDoS attack is a site or service suddenly becoming slow or unavailable. But since a number of causes, such as a legitimate spike in network traffic, can create similar performance issues, further investigation is usually required. Signs of a DDoS attack can be:

  • Suspicious amounts of traffic originating from single IP address or IP range
  • A flood of traffic from users who share the same device type
  • Odd traffic patterns such as spikes at odd hours of the day

Types of DDoS Attacks

  1. Volume-Based Attacks:
    • Goal: Consume the target's bandwidth.
    • Methods: UDP floods, ICMP floods (ping floods), or spoofed packet floods.
    • Example: Sending massive amounts of junk traffic to saturate the network.
    • Scale: Measured in bits per second (bps).
  2. Protocol Attacks:
    • Goal: Exploit weaknesses in the protocol stack to exhaust server resources.
    • Methods: SYN floods, fragmented packet attacks, or Smurf attacks.
    • Scale: Measured in packets per second (pps).
  3. Application Layer Attacks (Layer 7):
    • Goal: Target specific web applications or services.
    • Methods: HTTP floods or DNS query floods.
    • Scale: Measured in requests per second (rps).

Examples of DDoS Attacks

  • Mirai Botnet Attack (2016):
    • Targeted Dyn, a major DNS provider, disrupting services for companies like Twitter, Netflix, and Amazon.
    • Used IoT devices as bots, generating massive traffic.
  • GitHub Attack (2018):
    • The largest DDoS attack at the time, with traffic peaking at 1.3 Tbps.
    • Used memcached servers to amplify the attack.
